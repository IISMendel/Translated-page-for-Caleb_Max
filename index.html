The Canegrate Culture

Roots of the Proto-Celtic Identity in the Cisalpine Region

Cinerary urns from the Canegrate necropolis.

Historical Overview

The Canegrate culture represents a fundamental period in the proto-history of Northern Italy, specifically during the 13th century BC (Recent Bronze Age). It marks the emergence of a new cultural horizon characterized by the Urnfield rite, which would eventually evolve into the Iron Age Golasecca culture.

The Discovery

The site’s significance came to light in 1926 when engineer Guido Sutermeister discovered the first burial remains. Subsequent excavations between 1953 and 1956, led by Ferrante Rittatore Vonwiller, revealed a vast necropolis of approximately 165 tombs, making it one of the most important archaeological benchmarks in the Po Valley.

Bronze artifacts: A characteristic Canegrate-type sword.

A Proto-Celtic Civilization

Archaeologists classify this culture as Proto-Celtic. The material evidence, particularly the pottery and bronze weaponry, shows strong connections with the transalpine cultures of Central Europe. This indicates that the populations living in the area between the Olona and Ticino rivers were part of a large-scale European network of exchange and migration.

Material Culture and Burial Rites

The hallmark of the Canegrate culture is its funerary ritual: cremation. The ashes were placed in terracotta urns, often decorated with geometric patterns, and buried in simple pits. Unlike previous eras, the relative uniformity of the graves suggests a society with shared communal values and emerging social stratification.
